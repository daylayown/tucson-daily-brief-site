# Tucson Daily Brief — February 23, 2026

Tucson Daily Brief — February 23, 2026

🚨 Public Safety

Deadly pedestrian crash closes I-10 near Grant Road overnight. One person died Sunday night after being struck by a vehicle on westbound I-10 near the Grant Road exit (milepost 225). Arizona Department of Public Safety closed the highway for several hours, directing drivers to exit at Grant Road and re-enter at Miracle Mile; the roadway was reopened by 5:51 a.m. Monday. DPS has not released the victim's identity or further details as the investigation continues.

KGUN 9

Volunteer search groups comb area as Nancy Guthrie case hits three-week mark. Two volunteer groups — including Madres Buscadoras de Sonora, a binational group of mothers searching for missing children — joined the effort Sunday to find the missing Catalina Foothills woman. One group found a backpack that was reviewed by Pima County Sheriff's Department investigators but deemed not a viable lead; it appeared to have been outside far longer than three weeks. PCSD has placed temporary barricades on Camino Escalante to reduce traffic and media congestion near Guthrie's home. The reward for information leading to her recovery or arrest of a suspect now stands at $102,500 after an anonymous $100,000 donation to 88-CRIME.

🏛️ Government

Tucson leaders push back on cuts as city faces $27 million deficit. During a Wednesday study session, Mayor and Council reaffirmed their commitment to funding nonprofit housing partners and the city's public drug use prevention ordinance — even as city administrators outlined $9.1 million in possible savings to address the current fiscal year shortfall. Chief Financial Officer Anna Rosenberry presented four budget-cutting options; council members resisted proposals that would reduce general fund support for affordable housing programs and drug enforcement initiatives. The $9.1 million in potential cuts covers only a fraction of the year's gap.

Arizona Luminaria

RTA Next voter guide: March election sets Pima County's road priorities for years to come. The Regional Transportation Authority's $2.67 billion RTA Next plan goes to voters next month and will not raise taxes — but it determines which roads get funded over the next several years. Tucson Agenda notes that Silverbell Road remains cracked and crumbling, and Tangerine Road improvements are still delayed; the plan's fate will directly affect residents in Marana, Oro Valley, and unincorporated Pima County who rely on bus service and local arterials.

Tucson Agenda

Pima County Supervisors adopt policies restricting ICE activity on county property. Supervisors passed two new policies Tuesday limiting federal immigration enforcement agents from using county-owned property for operations and imposing a mask ban on law enforcement on county grounds. Supporters called the measures a clear message to federal agents; Republican Supervisor Christy dismissed the policies as "toothless." AZPM and Tucson Agenda both reported the policies represent a first step, with more measures potentially forthcoming.

AZPM | Tucson Agenda

Marana rejects data center referendum petitions. The Town of Marana has determined that petitions filed to force a public vote on a proposed data center do not meet the legal requirements under Arizona law. The decision clears the path for the data center to proceed without a community referendum, ending a months-long grassroots effort to bring the project to a ballot.

AZPM

Benson city council recall election set after aluminum plant opposition. Three Benson City Council members will face challengers in a recall election after months of grassroots opposition to an aluminum manufacturing plant currently under construction in the small Cochise County city southeast of Tucson. The recall campaign forced half of the council to defend their seats, reflecting significant community discontent over the project's environmental and economic implications.

🎓 Education

University of Arizona dismantles cultural housing communities, shifts to interdisciplinary model. A UA program that for years grouped students from similar cultural and ethnic backgrounds together in residence halls will no longer center on cultural identity. The university says it is rebranding the communities with an interdisciplinary focus. The change follows broader national pressure on universities to move away from race- and identity-based programming.

🏗️ Development & Business

Oro Valley stormwater utility fees rising in three-year phased increase. Stormwater utility fees will go up in Oro Valley beginning July 1 under a three-year phased rate hike approved by the town. The increases are designed to address infrastructure maintenance and stormwater management needs in the rapidly growing northern suburb.

Inside Tucson Business

Brandylion: Tea & Spirits opens in downtown Tucson at Presidio Museum site. The new tea and craft spirits bar opened Saturday, Feb. 21 at 200 N. Court Ave. at Presidio San Agustín del Tucson Museum — the space formerly occupied by Dandelion Cafe and Bakery, which relocated across the street. The opening adds a new venue to downtown Tucson's food and beverage scene.

La Fiesta de los Vaqueros goes pink for breast cancer awareness. Tucson's annual rodeo turned pink Sunday as organizers raised more than $7,000 for a local nonprofit supporting families affected by breast cancer. The event continues through March 1 at the Tucson Rodeo Grounds.
