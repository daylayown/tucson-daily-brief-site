Marana Town Council Meeting — March 17, 2026

Tucson Daily Brief Preview

This council meeting features two significant executive sessions tied to active lawsuits against the town, a public hearing on short-term rental regulations for accessory dwelling units, and early budget deliberations — making it one of the more consequential regular meetings of the year despite a relatively lean action agenda.

Top Stories to Watch

1. 🏛️ Council Gets Legal Strategy Briefing on Data Center Referendum Lawsuits

Executive Session Item E3 | NOT on consent agenda

The council will go into closed session to huddle with town attorneys on *two* active lawsuits — *Marana Citizens for Ethical and Transparent Government v. Town of Marana* (C20261567) and *Fremont Peak Properties v. Town of Marana* (C20261773) — both connected to the ongoing data center rezoning controversy. This is the clearest signal yet that the legal battle over the rejected referendum petitions is escalating on multiple fronts simultaneously, with both citizen groups and a developer now in Pima County Superior Court. While the session itself is closed to the public, any direction the council gives its attorneys could shape whether Marana settles, fights, or faces a potential reversal of the rezoning decision that set off the dispute.

2. ⚖️ Second Active Lawsuit Against the Town Gets Closed-Door Review

Executive Session Item E2 | NOT on consent agenda

Separately, the council will also meet in executive session over *Unrein v. Town of Marana* (C20254246), a distinct case that hasn't received as much public attention. The fact that Marana is juggling at least three active lawsuits discussed at a single meeting points to a broader pattern of legal exposure tied to the town's aggressive development posture. Residents and taxpayers should want to know what this litigation is costing — and whether it connects to the same data center and land use controversies dominating local debate.

3. 🏠 Public Hearing: Should Marana ADUs Be Allowed as Airbnbs?

Council Action Item A1 — Public Hearing | NOT on consent agenda

The council will hold a public hearing on changes to the town's accessory dwelling unit (ADU) ordinance, specifically clarifying when — and whether — ADUs can be operated as short-term rentals. The proposal also revises the definition of "restricted-affordable dwelling unit" and updates rules for properties near the airport. This matters because ADUs have been pitched regionwide as a tool to ease the housing crunch, and allowing them to be converted to Airbnbs instead could undercut that goal; how Marana draws this line will be watched by housing advocates and vacation rental operators across the Tucson metro.

4. 💰 FY2027 Budget Season Opens: Where Is Marana Spending Next Year?

Discussion Item D2 | NOT on consent agenda

Town Manager Terry Rozema will present proposed budget initiatives and expenditures for fiscal year 2026–2027, kicking off the formal budget development process. With Marana continuing rapid growth along the I-10 corridor — and facing mounting legal costs, water infrastructure demands, and transportation needs — the priorities signaled here will telegraph whether the town is investing in services that keep pace with its population or stretching thin. This is the first public look at the town's spending blueprint and worth tracking for any surprises in staffing, capital projects, or fee changes.

5. 🚧 Tangerine Interchange Outreach Plan Signals Major Construction Ahead

Presentation Item P1 | NOT on consent agenda

Staff will present the town's community outreach strategy for upcoming improvements to the Tangerine Road interchange, a critical I-10 chokepoint for tens of thousands of daily commuters in Marana and north Tucson. The fact that the town is formalizing a communications plan suggests construction timelines are getting real — and anyone living or working near Tangerine, or using it as an I-10 on-ramp, will want to know what's coming and when. It's also worth asking how this project interfaces with the Regional Transportation Authority's broader Tucson-area priorities.

6. 🏗️ Grading Permit Rules Tightened as Development Boom Continues

Consent Agenda Item C1 — Ordinance 2026.006

⚠️ On consent agenda — could pass without discussion.

A proposed ordinance would amend Marana's grading and site work rules, adding new provisions for permit expiration, extensions, reactivation, and — notably — "at-risk permit revocations" for major grading projects. In a town where large residential and commercial pads are being graded constantly along the I-10 corridor, these procedural changes could affect how quickly (or slowly) developers can move dirt, and give the town new tools to pull permits on stalled or problematic projects. Worth flagging given how often grading activity precedes controversial development approvals in Marana.
