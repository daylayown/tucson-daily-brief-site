Pima County Board of Supervisors: July 14, 2026 — Meeting Preview

This is a consequential meeting for Pima County residents, with major decisions pending on political leadership, border policy, and funding for dozens of social service nonprofits. The board will also take up a $25 million wastewater infrastructure contract and make an appointment to fill the vacant County Treasurer position.

Top Stories to Watch

1. Board Must Fill Vacant County Treasurer Seat — and Pick Its Next Top Administrator

Item 22 | Item 21 | Not on consent calendar

The board will appoint a new Pima County Treasurer — a position that manages billions in public funds — while simultaneously deciding how to conduct the search for a new County Administrator, the top staff executive who runs day-to-day county operations. Having two of the county's most powerful administrative roles in flux simultaneously raises real questions about continuity and governance at a time when the county faces budget pressures. Residents should watch for whether the board appoints an insider or outsider to the Treasurer post, and whether supervisors can agree on a transparent public process for the administrator search.

2. County Takes Formal Stand Against Border Wall on Tohono O'odham Nation

Item 24 | Not on consent calendar

The board will vote on Resolution 2026-37, formally opposing President Trump's executive order to build a border wall through the Tohono O'odham Nation's lands. The resolution — submitted by District 3 Supervisor — puts Pima County on record in direct conflict with a federal executive order, continuing the county's pattern of resistance to Trump-era border enforcement policies. The Tohono O'odham Nation's reservation straddles the U.S.-Mexico border for roughly 75 miles, and tribal leaders have long opposed wall construction on their sovereign land; this vote signals where the county stands in that ongoing fight.

3. County to Cut Millions From Social Service Nonprofits — Dozens of Programs at Risk

Item 26 | Not on consent calendar

The board will vote on Outside Agency Committee funding recommendations for FY 2026/27 — and the numbers are stark. Agencies requested nearly $10.1 million in total funding, but the committee recommends approving only $3.9 million — a cut of roughly 61%. Major reductions include the Community Food Bank (from $375,000 to $262,873 for emergency food assistance), Emerge Center Against Domestic Abuse (from $325,000 to $115,798), and Catholic Community Services. Several programs — including Tucson Medical Center's Desert Kids Safety Program, the UA's Native American Health Center (which requested $2.69 million), and St. Luke's nutritional services — received zero funding. For residents who rely on food banks, homeless services, child care, and domestic violence support, these cuts come at a difficult moment and could mean reduced capacity across Tucson's safety net.

4. $25 Million Wastewater Contract Could Shape County Infrastructure for Years

Item 29 | Not on consent calendar

The board will consider awarding a $25 million job-order contract to four construction firms — B & F Contracting, Borderland Construction, Ellison-Mills, and Hunter Contracting — for repair, rehabilitation, and construction of the county's wastewater conveyance system. Job-order contracts allow the county to issue task orders for work as needs arise, meaning this isn't one project but a pipeline of infrastructure work funded through the Regional Wastewater Reclamation Department. At $25 million, this is one of the largest contracts on today's agenda and will directly affect the reliability of sewer service for hundreds of thousands of county residents.

5. County Buying Flood-Prone Land in Brawley Wash Area — With Multiple Related Items

Items 10, 11, 12 | Board sitting as Flood Control District | Not on consent calendar

The board, sitting as the Regional Flood Control District, will consider three related actions on the Brawley Wash watershed: purchasing approximately 28.86 acres of vacant flood-prone land for $555,000 (Item 11), extending a $593,247 engineering contract for bank restoration design work on the Drexel-to-Valencia corridor (Item 10), and extending a USDA watershed restoration planning grant (Item 12). Taken together, these items represent a sustained, multi-year county investment in flood mitigation in Tucson's southwest corridor — a flood-vulnerable area where undeveloped land acquisition is a key strategy to reduce property damage and protect lives during monsoon events.

6. Search and Rescue Contract in Limbo — Funding Source Unknown

Item 23 | Not on consent calendar

The board will consider renewing the Southern Arizona Rescue Association contract for $100,000 through June 2027 — but with an unusual catch: the funding source is listed as "to be identified at the discretion of the County Administrator." SARA provides critical search and rescue services across Pima County's vast wilderness and mountain terrain, responding to hikers, lost persons, and backcountry emergencies. The fact that the board is being asked to approve a contract without an identified funding source suggests budget strain and could become a flashpoint in the meeting, particularly given the ongoing County Administrator vacancy.

7. Development Impact Fee Audit Opens Window on Road Funding Health

Item 19 | Public Hearing | Not on consent calendar

State law requires a biennial public audit of how Pima County collects and spends roadway development impact fees — charges assessed on new construction to fund roads made necessary by growth. This public hearing gives residents and developers a rare chance to scrutinize whether the fees are being collected, tracked, and spent appropriately. With development pressure continuing in Marana, Sahuarita, and the I-10 corridor, the audit's findings have implications for whether existing residents end up subsidizing infrastructure costs that should fall on new development.

8. Wastewater Service Boundary Shift Could Open Door to Hundreds of New Homes Near Marana

Item 28 | Consent Calendar ⚠️

On the consent calendar — could pass without discussion. The board will consider updating the boundary map between the Town of Marana's and Pima County's Designated Management Agency (DMA) zones for wastewater service, specifically to allow Marana to extend sewer service to Saguaro Bloom Blocks 9 and 10 — a large master-planned community in the northwest. DMA boundaries determine which jurisdiction provides wastewater service to new development, and changes can effectively green-light hundreds of new homes. Because this is on the consent calendar, it could be approved with no public comment or board discussion, despite its land-use significance.
