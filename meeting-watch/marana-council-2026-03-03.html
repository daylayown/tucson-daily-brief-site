Marana Town Council Meeting — March 3, 2026: What to Watch

This is a relatively light agenda for Marana's fast-growing town council, but two financially significant items and a notable state technology grant deserve attention. Conspicuously absent are any agenda items directly addressing the ongoing data center rezoning controversy or the proposed ICE detention facility — topics likely to draw public comment during the Call to the Public.

Top Items to Watch

1. Town Fees Are Going Up — Here's What Could Cost You More

Item P1 — Presentation on Comprehensive Fee Schedule Changes | Not on consent agenda

Town Finance Director Yiannis Kalaitzidis will present proposed changes to Marana's fee schedule, covering a wide range of municipal charges that could affect residents, developers, and businesses. Fee schedule updates in fast-growing communities like Marana frequently include increases to development impact fees, utility rates, permit costs, and parks and recreation charges — costs that ripple through housing prices and everyday services. This is a discussion and direction item, meaning the council will signal what changes are acceptable before a formal vote at a future meeting, making tonight a key opportunity for public input.

2. Marana Creates New Financing District for Cascada Development

Item D2 — Resolution No. 2026-016: Formation of Cascada Community Facilities District | Council action required

The council will vote on whether to formally create a Community Facilities District (CFD) for Cascada, a large mixed-use development in Marana. CFDs are a financing mechanism that allows developers to fund public infrastructure — roads, water lines, sewers — by issuing bonds repaid through property tax assessments levied on future residents and property owners within the district. This matters because Tucson-area homebuyers have increasingly found themselves purchasing in CFDs without fully understanding the additional tax burden, and Marana has used this tool repeatedly as it races to build infrastructure for rapid I-10 corridor growth. The vote tonight on "ordering and declaring formation" is a foundational step that locks in the district's legal existence.

3. State Grant Funds New Police Records System — But What Data Gets Shared?

Item C1 — Resolution No. 2026-015: ADOA Agreement for Law Enforcement Records Management and Data Sharing System | On consent agenda

Marana Police Department is accepting a state grant through the Arizona Department of Administration to fund a new records management and data sharing system. While the grant funding itself is routine, the "data sharing" component warrants scrutiny — particularly given the ongoing national conversation about local law enforcement data sharing with federal immigration authorities like ICE. With a proposed ICE detention facility already on the table in Marana, residents and civil liberties advocates will want to know exactly what data is shared, with whom, and under what circumstances. Because it's on the consent agenda, it could pass without discussion unless a council member pulls it.

4. Development Pipeline Update: What's Coming to Marana?

Item D1 — Public and Private Projects Update | Council action item

Town Development Director Jason Angell will deliver the recurring update on Marana's active development projects and applications. While this is a standing agenda item, it serves as the clearest public window into the town's explosive growth along the I-10 corridor — new subdivisions, commercial projects, and infrastructure proposals that collectively shape traffic, water demand, and housing supply across the greater Tucson metro. Watch for any new applications or status changes on projects that connect to the data center controversy or large residential developments drawing regional attention.

What's Missing — And Why That Matters

The data center controversy — in which petitioners seeking a public referendum were rejected by the town — involves active litigation, which may explain why it's being handled in Executive Session (E1) rather than publicly. Residents with concerns about either issue should plan to speak during Call to the Public, the only open forum available to them tonight.
