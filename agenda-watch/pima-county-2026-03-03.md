# Pima County Board of Supervisors — March 3, 2026
### Meeting Preview: Tucson Daily Brief

This Tuesday's Board of Supervisors meeting is packed with consequential decisions, from a politically charged ordinance targeting masked protest groups to a $2 million homeless prevention grant and a 10-year housing strategy for the region. Supervisors will also receive updates on the county's finances and the ongoing RTA special election — making this one of the more substantive meetings of the year.

---

## Top Items to Watch

---

### 1. County Poised to Ban Unidentified Masked Groups in Public — Civil Liberties Groups Pushing Back
**Item 13 | Public Hearing | Unfinished Business**

The Board will hold a public hearing on Ordinance No. 2026-2, which would add a new chapter to county code regulating "unidentified masked groups" in public spaces, with an exemption carved out for law enforcement. This is returning as unfinished business, suggesting prior controversy or unresolved questions, and the agenda includes a community letter submitted just days before the meeting — a sign of active public opposition. This ordinance arrives in a charged national climate around protest rights and immigration enforcement, and civil liberties advocates are likely to show up in force; the outcome could set a significant legal and political precedent for Pima County.

> ⚠️ **Public Hearing** — community members may testify.

---

### 2. How Should Pima County Spend Its "Project Blue" Land Sale Windfall?
**Item 5 | Addendum | Unfinished Business**

The Board will decide how to allocate proceeds from the Project Blue land sale — a significant one-time revenue source — with staff recommendations and at least one community letter already submitted pushing for specific uses. With the county facing ongoing structural budget pressures, how supervisors choose to deploy this money (one-time infrastructure fix? housing? reserves?) is a major fiscal decision that connects directly to the budget deficit storyline. The fact that it's listed as unfinished business and has multiple competing memos signals genuine disagreement about priorities.

---

### 3. County Adopts 10-Year Regional Housing Strategy — With Developer and Realtor Pushback
**Item 22 | Regular Agenda**

Staff is recommending adoption of a sweeping 10-year Regional Housing Strategy and Funding Plan — and the agenda already includes comment letters from both the Southern Arizona Home Builders Association (SAHBA) and the Tucson Association of Realtors (TAR), suggesting the real estate industry has objections. Housing affordability is one of the defining issues for Tucson-area residents right now, and a decade-long county plan will shape zoning, subsidies, and development patterns across the region. Watch for whether supervisors adopt the plan as written or make concessions to industry groups.

---

### 4. Monthly Financial Update: How Deep Is the Hole?
**Item 24 | Regular Agenda**

The county's finance staff will present a monthly budget performance update through Period 7 (February), covering the current fiscal year's trajectory. Pima County has been navigating a structural budget deficit, and this mid-year snapshot will reveal whether revenues are tracking projections or whether the county is heading into crisis territory before the next budget cycle begins. Any shortfall numbers disclosed here will drive headlines and frame the coming months of budget negotiations.

---

### 5. $2 Million Private Grant for Homeless Prevention — Quietly on Consent
**Consent Item 15 | Consent Calendar**

The Garcia Family Foundation is giving Pima County $2 million over nearly three years to fund the county's Homelessness Prevention Hub — a substantial private philanthropic investment in one of the county's most urgent social challenges. The fact that this is buried on the consent calendar means it could pass with zero discussion, despite being one of the largest single grants on today's agenda. Residents should know this kind of funding exists and how the county plans to deploy it.

> 📋 **On consent calendar** — likely to pass without discussion.

---

### 6. Nearly $1.9 Million in Foster Care Legal Services — Also Sliding Through on Consent
**Consent Item 18 | Consent Calendar**

The county's Public Defense Services department is accepting nearly $1.9 million in federal Title IV-E funds to provide legal representation for children and parents in foster care proceedings. This is a significant investment in the county's public defense infrastructure for one of its most vulnerable populations, and it connects to broader concerns about the child welfare system's capacity under federal funding uncertainty. It deserves public awareness even if supervisors don't debate it.

> 📋 **On consent calendar** — likely to pass without discussion.

---

### 7. RTA Special Election Update: Mail Ballots, Votemobile, and Pima's Election Debut
**Addendum Item 4 | Time Certain: 10:15 a.m.**

The Elections Director and County Recorder will brief supervisors on Pima County's first-ever all-mail countywide election, held in connection with the Regional Transportation Authority special election — and will specifically discuss the Recorder's new "Votemobile" and how it's being deployed. This is a live story: how the election is being run, whether turnout is meeting expectations, and whether the Votemobile is reaching underserved communities are all questions voters care about ahead of what could be a billion-dollar transportation funding decision.

---

### 8. County Names a Wildlife Corridor — And It Matters for Development
**Addendum Item 3 | Time Certain: 10:00 a.m.**

The Board will vote on Resolution No. 2026-12, formally designating the Avra Valley landscape linkage in the Conservation Lands System as the "William Shaw Wildlife Corridor." While naming resolutions can be ceremonial, formally designating a wildlife corridor in the Conservation Lands System carries real land-use implications — it strengthens the legal and planning framework for protecting that landscape from future development encroachment west of Tucson. This connects to ongoing debates about growth, water, and habitat preservation in the Sonoran Desert.
