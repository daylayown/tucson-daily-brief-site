### 2. County Takes Formal Stand Against Border Wall on Tohono O'odham Nation

### 3. County to Cut Millions From Social Service Nonprofits — Dozens of Programs at Risk

**Item 26 | Not on consent calendar**

The board will vote on Outside Agency Committee funding recommendations for FY 2026/27 — and the numbers are stark. Agencies requested nearly **$10.1 million** in total funding, but the committee recommends approving only **$3.9 million** — a cut of roughly 61%. Major reductions include the Community Food Bank (from $375,000 to $262,873 for emergency food assistance), Emerge Center Against Domestic Abuse (from $325,000 to $115,798), and Catholic Community Services. Several programs — including Tucson Medical Center's Desert Kids Safety Program, the UA's Native American Health Center (which requested $2.69 million), and St. Luke's nutritional services — received **zero funding**. For residents who rely on food banks, homeless services, child care, and domestic violence support, these cuts come at a difficult moment and could mean reduced capacity across Tucson's safety net.

### 4. $25 Million Wastewater Contract Could Shape County Infrastructure for Years

**Item 29 | Not on consent calendar**

The board will consider awarding a **$25 million job-order contract** to four construction firms — B & F Contracting, Borderland Construction, Ellison-Mills, and Hunter Contracting — for repair, rehabilitation, and construction of the county's wastewater conveyance system. Job-order contracts allow the county to issue task orders for work as needs arise, meaning this isn't one project but a pipeline of infrastructure work funded through the Regional Wastewater Reclamation Department. At $25 million, this is one of the largest contracts on today's agenda and will directly affect the reliability of sewer service for hundreds of thousands of county residents.

The board will consider renewing the **Southern Arizona Rescue Association** contract for $100,000 through June 2027 — but with an unusual catch: the funding source is listed as "to be identified at the discretion of the County Administrator." ### 7. Development Impact Fee Audit Opens Window on Road Funding Health

**Item 19 | Public Hearing | Not on consent calendar**

State law requires a biennial public audit of how Pima County collects and spends **roadway development impact fees** — charges assessed on new construction to fund roads made necessary by growth. This public hearing gives residents and developers a rare chance to scrutinize whether the fees are being collected, tracked, and spent appropriately. With development pressure continuing in Marana, Sahuarita, and the I-10 corridor, the audit's findings have implications for whether existing residents end up subsidizing infrastructure costs that should fall on new development.

### 8. Wastewater Service Boundary Shift Could Open Door to Hundreds of New Homes Near Marana

**Item 28 | Consent Calendar ⚠️**

**On the consent calendar — could pass without discussion.** The board will consider updating the boundary map between the Town of Marana's and Pima County's Designated Management Agency (DMA) zones for wastewater service, specifically to allow Marana to extend sewer service to **Saguaro Bloom Blocks 9 and 10** — a large master-planned community in the northwest. DMA boundaries determine which jurisdiction provides wastewater service to new development, and changes can effectively green-light hundreds of new homes. Because this is on the consent calendar, it could be approved with no public comment or board discussion, despite its land-use significance.
